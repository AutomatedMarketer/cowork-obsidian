## 1 🎯 WHAT THIS PROJECT GIVES YOU

By the end of this project you'll have a local-first second brain: a folder of plain markdown files on your computer that holds your thinking across work, health, family, business, and whatever else matters — organized so Claude can read it, cross-link it, and keep it up to date for you. You write freely into a `raw/` drawer. Claude builds and maintains a clean `wiki/` from what's in raw. Deliverables from collaborations go into `output/`. And because it's all plain text on your disk, you own it forever — no cloud service can take it away.

**Five lessons.** Each one gives your second brain a new capability:

1. **Connect or create your vault** — install the plugin, run `/onboard-second-brain`, detect a vault you already use OR scaffold a fresh one. Wired to Cowork in under 15 minutes.
2. **The three operations** — learn `/second-brain build`, `/second-brain update`, `/second-brain health-check`. Run each one once.
3. **Sync across machines** — pick the right sync option (Obsidian Sync, Syncthing, or Git/GitHub) so your vault is everywhere you work. Optional: also wire it to Claude Desktop via MCP.
4. **Weekly rhythm** — install calendar reminders, learn `/open-vault` as your daily-driver command, write `My Vault System.md`, set up home indexes.
5. **Use cases & daily workflow** — concrete examples of how cohort members use this in their actual day-to-day. The "now what?" guide.

## 2 🧠 THE BIG IDEA (READ THIS FIRST — IT'S SHORT)

Most people build their "second brain" in ChatGPT or Notion AI. That breaks in three ways:

1. **Recency bias.** Cloud memory leans on recent chats and forgets older context.
2. **Silos.** Each Project or GPT is its own little world. Crossing them means re-uploading context every time.
3. **Someone else's servers.** If pricing, terms, or availability change, your second brain moves with them — or vanishes.

A local second brain fixes all three. Every note is a plain `.md` file on your disk. Any AI that can read local files can use it. No lock-in. No subscription. If Obsidian shuts down tomorrow, the files stay. If Claude raises prices, you swap in a different assistant that reads the same folder.

The model is deceptively simple: **raw / wiki / output**.

- **Raw** is where you write. Brain dumps, journal entries, article clippings, screenshot OCR, half-formed ideas.
- **Wiki** is where Claude writes. Short, focused pages — one topic per page, cross-linked. You don't touch the wiki. You can't mess it up if you don't touch it.
- **Output** is where you and Claude together put the good stuff that came out of a session. A strategy doc. A client guide. A distilled framework.

Julie Chenell frames it well: *"There's a human in raw, a robot in wiki, and a human AND a robot in output."*

Over time, raw fills up with your thinking, wiki gets richer, output accumulates real deliverables, and every new Claude session becomes sharper because it can read everything you've ever written — without you re-uploading anything.

## 3 🔌 THE TWO TOOLS YOU'LL USE

| TOOL | WHAT IT IS | WHY THIS ONE |
|---|---|---|
| **Obsidian** | A free local-first note app. Every note is a plain `.md` file on disk. | Makes editing markdown pleasant without locking you in. The folder of files is still just a folder of files. |
| **Claude Cowork** *(already set up from Project 01)* | The Co-Work tab. Reads and writes files in your vault folder. | Builds and maintains the wiki for you, runs health checks, produces outputs. |

The vault is the source of truth. Obsidian and Cowork are just two ways to view the same folder. If either tool disappears tomorrow, your vault stays yours.

### What `cowork-obsidian` adds — three new Cowork commands

When you install the `cowork-obsidian` plugin, Cowork gets **three new slash commands**. That's the whole "app" — three commands. Memorize these:

| Command | When to use it | How often |
|---|---|---|
| `/onboard-second-brain` (or just `start onboarding`) | First-time setup. Walks you through connecting an existing vault or scaffolding a fresh one, then wiring it to Cowork. | Once. |
| `/open-vault` (or `open my vault`) | Every time you sit down to work on your second brain. Launches Obsidian to your vault AND preps the Cowork session — vault status, last activity, what you should do next. | Daily / whenever you start a session. |
| `/second-brain` (or `update my wiki`, `build my [area] wiki`, etc.) | The operational skill. Four operations: `build`, `update`, `health-check`, `fold-back`. | Weekly (`update`), monthly (`health-check`), quarterly (`fold-back`), once-per-area (`build`). |

The lessons in this project install the plugin (Lesson 1) and then teach you how to use the three commands (Lessons 2–4). Lesson 5 covers concrete use cases — what real cohort users do with this on a daily basis.

> **About connecting Cowork to your vault:** there's no MCP server to install for the Cowork path. Cowork already has filesystem access — pointing it at your vault folder is the entire connection. If you *also* want Claude **Desktop** (the chat app, not Cowork) to see the vault, that's an optional extra in Lesson 3 — and it does need an MCP server.

## 4 🆕 WHAT'S NEW IN v0.2.0 (vs the original SOPs)

If you ran Project 04 before with the original `cowork-second-brain` plugin, here's what changed:

- **Plugin renamed.** It's now `cowork-obsidian`. The slash commands `/onboard-second-brain` and `/second-brain` are unchanged — same muscle memory.
- **Connect-existing branch.** The old install assumed you had no vault. Now Lesson 1 starts by auto-detecting any Obsidian vault you already opened on this machine. If you've got one, you connect to it without reorganizing it. If you don't, we scaffold a fresh one.
- **Three vault modes.** Every vault now has a `mode`: `external` (existing vault, don't touch the structure), `overlay` (existing vault + raw/wiki/output added on one sub-folder), or `scaffold` (built from scratch, the original v0.1.0 model).
- **Sync is its own lesson.** Obsidian Sync, Syncthing, or Git/GitHub — pick one based on a 2-question decision tree.
- **Optional Claude Desktop MCP.** Cohort path stays Cowork-only. Power users can also wire Claude Desktop to the vault.
- **Soft prerequisite on `cowork-ai-os`.** If you don't have it, the plugin works standalone with a minimal safe-zones file.

If you ran v0.1.0 already, your existing state file auto-migrates (assumes `mode = scaffold`, prompts once for confirmation). No data loss.

## 5 📊 THE WORKFLOW IN ONE PICTURE

```
       YOU                CLAUDE COWORK            YOU + CLAUDE
        │                       │                       │
        ▼                       ▼                       ▼
      raw/  ──build/update──▶  wiki/  ──reads──▶  conversation  ──save──▶  output/
        ▲                                                                     │
        └─────────────────── fold back in ────────────────────────────────────┘
```

- Daily or weekly: drop raw material into `raw/`.
- Every week or two: tell Claude to build or update the wiki from what's new in raw.
- Monthly: tell Claude to run a health check — flag contradictions, missing topics, stale pages.
- Any time you produce something great: save it to `output/`.
- Occasionally (quarterly): tell Claude to fold outputs back into the wiki so the next session can reference them.

**Three prompts, forever.** You'll write them once in Lesson 2 and re-use them for years.

## 6 📁 THE FOLDER SHAPE YOU'RE BUILDING (scaffold mode)

If you're scaffolding a fresh vault in Lesson 1 (no existing Obsidian vault on your machine), the empty shell looks like this by the end:

```
<vault-root>/
├── about-me/
│   ├── About Me.md
│   ├── Brand Voice.md
│   ├── AI Working Style.md
│   └── My Vault System.md           (written in Lesson 4)
├── <life-area-1>/    e.g. work-life/
│   ├── raw/
│   ├── wiki/
│   └── output/
├── <life-area-2>/    e.g. business/
│   ├── raw/
│   ├── wiki/
│   └── output/
├── <life-area-3>/    e.g. health/
│   ├── raw/
│   ├── wiki/
│   └── output/
└── <life-area-4>/    e.g. personal/
    ├── raw/
    ├── wiki/
    └── output/
```

Four rules make this work:

- **One vault.** Not five. Claude needs one place to look.
- **Maximum four life areas.** Over four and paths get confusing for you and for Claude. Macro beats micro.
- **Exactly three subfolders per life area.** Always `raw`, `wiki`, `output`. Same structure everywhere — predictable paths let prompts generalize.
- **One top-level `about-me/`** with your identity files. Loaded every session, just like your Cowork workspace version.

> **If you connect to an existing vault** (external or overlay mode), the shape is whatever you already have. You don't need to reorganize anything. Lesson 1 covers both paths.

## 10 ⚠ A NOTE ON THE MINDSET

Building a second brain is not a ten-minute setup and then "done." It's a rhythm. You'll write into raw often. You'll ask Claude to build/update the wiki every week or two. Every month you'll run a health check. The value compounds over years, not weeks.

If you're looking for an immediate "magic tool," this isn't it. If you're looking for a system that makes you smarter for the next ten years, this is exactly it.

## 11 🎓 TEACHING NOTES (FOR THE INSTRUCTOR)

**Timing:** the whole project lands at roughly 100 minutes if students don't get philosophical about their life areas. If they do, Lesson 1 can stretch to 60+ minutes alone. Connect-existing students finish much faster.

**Live demo:** show a populated vault (one with real raw material and a real wiki) before students build their own. Seeing the end state first makes the structure feel purposeful, not theoretical.

**Emphasis:** *"Raw is what you do, wiki is what the AI does, output is what happens when you and AI collaborate."* That Julie Chenell line is the single most portable framing — steal it.

**Watch for:**

- Students who already have an Obsidian vault and assume they need to scaffold a new one. **Push them to connect their existing vault** in `external` mode — Lesson 1 walks the auto-detection.
- Students who want to bulk-import their ChatGPT history. **Push back** — start fresh, pull specific valuable pieces into raw as they're needed.
- Students who over-split into eight life areas. **Push them back down to four.** Subfolders inside `raw/` are fine for fine-grained topics.
- Students who start writing in the wiki by hand. That's AI territory. Wiki hands always come from raw via Claude.
- Students who hesitate on Lesson 3 because "they only have one computer." **Skip it.** Sync is optional; they can come back if they ever add a second machine.
