# Use Cases — Why You're Setting This Up, and What You Actually Do With It

A second brain in Obsidian + Claude Cowork is a thinking system, not a productivity gadget. This doc answers two questions every new customer asks:

1. **Why am I setting this up?** Most people use ChatGPT, Notion, or another cloud-based tool. Each fails in three ways:
   - **Recency bias** — cloud memory leans on recent chats and forgets older context.
   - **Silos** — each Project or GPT is its own little world. Crossing them means re-uploading.
   - **Someone else's servers** — if pricing, terms, or availability change, your second brain moves with them or vanishes.
2. **What do I do with it once it's set up?** You write into a `raw/` drawer freely. Claude builds and maintains a clean `wiki/` from what's in raw. You and Claude collaborate on real deliverables in `output/`. Then you re-run three prompts on a weekly rhythm — `build`, `update`, `health-check` — for years.

Julie Chenell's framing: *"There's a human in raw, a robot in wiki, and a human AND a robot in output."*

The result: a thinking apparatus that compounds for years instead of a chatbot that forgets last Tuesday.

## 6 case studies (drawn from VCInc cohort SOPs and the existing v0.1.0 install)

Each case study covers: who you are → what hurts before the second brain → what goes into `raw/` → what Claude builds in `wiki/` → real deliverables in `output/` → time/value created.

### Case Study 1 — Agency Owner: Client deliverables on demand

*Persona:* Agency owner (solopreneur or small team)

*Pain:* Re-uploading client briefs, past project context, and objection-handling templates into ChatGPT for every new project. Knowledge scattered across Notion, email, and old project files. Client strategies that worked three years ago are lost.

*raw flow:* Drop client call transcripts, project briefs, past email threads, and "objections we keep seeing" into `raw/` every week. Screenshot key client feedback, paste competitor analysis notes, save win/loss debrief notes.

*wiki output:* Claude builds focused pages — *"recurring client objections + how we've answered them," "project templates by service type," "winning positioning statements," "client profiles by industry."* Cross-linked, so when you land a new healthcare client, you immediately see the healthcare playbook.

*output value:* Strategy decks, client onboarding docs, service positioning guides — all draft-ready because they reference the wiki. A proposal that would have taken 6 hours now takes 90 minutes because the framework, objection-handling, and past positioning live in one indexed place.

*Time saved / value created:* New client projects move 3× faster because context is structured, indexed, and instantly available — no email search, no *"where did we put the last healthcare strategy."*

*Sources: SOP-C04-00 (overview), SOP-C04-03 (weekly rhythm + outputs)*

### Case Study 2 — Coach: Distilled client frameworks

*Persona:* Business coach or performance coach

*Pain:* Each client session you answer the same core questions (managing energy, scaling team, handling objections). Insights from 50 past clients live in memory or scattered notes. Re-discovering lessons every time a new client hits a familiar block.

*raw flow:* After every client call: brain dump the key insight, the objection they faced, your answer, the emotional context. Add case examples — *"client ABC scaled from 6-figure to 7-figure by doing X," "client DEF avoided Y."* Clip articles or research you reference frequently.

*wiki output:* Claude distills these into repeatable frameworks — *"The Energy Management Playbook," "Scaling a Team from 5 to 15: The 3 Bottlenecks," "How to Handle the 'I Don't Have Time' Objection."* Each page cross-links to related client stories, making your coaching feel personalized but draws on deep patterns.

*output value:* Coaching guides, email sequences you send clients between sessions, *"here's what we work on in month 1"* documents that cite the patterns you've seen across 30 clients. Every new client feels like they're getting the wisdom of your whole practice, not just this month's thinking.

*Time saved / value created:* Client coaching calls become sharper and faster because you're pulling from a 50-client knowledge base in real time, not replaying the same explanations every cycle.

*Sources: SOP-C04-00 (coaching life area), SOP-C04-02 (distilled topic pages), SOP-C04-03 (recurring objection responses)*

### Case Study 3 — Course Creator: Evergreen curriculum on demand

*Persona:* Course creator, online educator, cohort instructor

*Pain:* Teaching the same module three times a year. Each time you re-record or re-write from scratch. Student FAQs repeat. Lessons are scattered across Notion, Airtable, video scripts, and old email threads. Your best answers to tough questions live in chat history, not anywhere reusable.

*raw flow:* As you teach, dump: student questions that stump you, misconceptions you keep correcting, analogies that finally click for people. Save recordings or transcripts, clip articles you assign, add *"note to self"* improvements. After each cohort, brain-dump what landed, what flopped, what students asked for next.

*wiki output:* Claude builds — *"Module 2: FAQ by confusion pattern," "The 5 misconceptions about X and how I've addressed them in past cohorts," "Here are the analogies that actually work," "Lesson 3 prerequisites: make sure students understand A, B, C first."* Every module is distilled from 2–3 years of teaching the same content.

*output value:* Next cohort curriculum, recorded module intros (*"here's what confuses people most in this section"*), email sequences for between-lessons reinforcement, student success guides. Every artifact is drawn from real student patterns, making them feel less like generic course material and more like *"here's what I've learned teaching this 100 times."*

*Time saved / value created:* Fourth cohort costs 30% of the first because structure, FAQ handling, and success patterns are indexed and reusable instead of reinvented each cycle.

*Sources: SOP-C04-00 (teaching), SOP-C04-03 (recurring patterns + golden outputs)*

### Case Study 4 — Consultant: Research compound across years

*Persona:* Strategy consultant, research-heavy solopreneur, analyst

*Pain:* Researching the same market, the same competitive set, the same client-type problems every few months. Competitive intelligence lives in bookmarks, scattered reports, and old client docs. You write the same market summary for every new client brief.

*raw flow:* As you research: paste industry reports, clip competitor websites, save market data, add your observations (*"noticed X company shifted strategy in March"*). For each client engagement: dump the brief, your diagnostic notes, client feedback, market assumptions. Save it all raw and unpolished.

*wiki output:* Claude maintains — *"Market overview (last 6 months of changes)," "Competitor playbook by type," "Common client assumptions that are wrong," "Success metrics that matter in this space."* The wiki stays evergreen — each new raw research note triggers an `update` that keeps competitive intelligence fresh.

*output value:* Client strategy decks that cite the indexed competitive landscape, market positioning memos that reference the wiki's distilled competitor analysis, diagnostic reports that show you know the space — because you do, and the wiki proves it.

*Time saved / value created:* Proposal-to-strategy-draft drops from 20 hours to 5 because research is cumulative across clients instead of starting over with each engagement.

*Sources: SOP-C04-00 (consulting / research), SOP-C04-02 (update keeping data fresh), SOP-C04-03 (quarterly fold-back of deliverables into wiki)*

### Case Study 5 — Content Creator / Writer: Long-form ideas from brain dumps

*Persona:* Solopreneur writer, newsletter author, YouTube creator, journalist

*Pain:* Half-formed ideas scattered across voice notes, Twitter drafts, phone photos, Roam Research, three different note apps. When you sit down to write a newsletter or script, you remember you have good material but can't find it. Context-building takes 3 hours for what should be 45 minutes.

*raw flow:* Every day: jot observations, clip articles you read, paste reader feedback, record half-thoughts. Journal about what you're seeing in the market. Screenshot social posts that resonate. Dump voice-recorded ideas (transcribed) without editing. Let it be messy.

*wiki output:* Claude organizes — *"Recurring reader questions about X," "The 3 narrative threads I keep returning to," "Evergreen ideas worth revisiting," "Competitor newsletter patterns."* The wiki becomes your idea index — sorted by topic, with cross-links so you see how ideas connect.

*output value:* Newsletter drafts, YouTube script outlines, Twitter threads — all pulled from the wiki because it shows you which ideas have the most weight in your thinking. A 2000-word post that would have required 2 hours of material-gathering now needs 20 minutes of wiki-skimming plus 40 minutes of writing.

*Time saved / value created:* Content output increases 40% because the thinking is indexed and available, not buried in a vault of 200 unorganized notes.

*Sources: SOP-C04-00 (writing / personal), SOP-C04-01 (brain dumps, journal entries, article clippings), SOP-C04-03 (golden outputs like finished pieces)*

### Case Study 6 — Multi-Role Operator: Work + Business + Health as one system

*Persona:* Solopreneur running multiple businesses, coach/consultant/creator hybrid, founder wearing many hats

*Pain:* Context-switching between roles. Work notes in one app, business strategy in another, health habits in email. No unified place to see how decisions in one area affect others. Using separate second-brain systems means re-uploading context across tools.

*raw flow:* Four life areas — `work` (day job / client work), `business` (your own product / service), `health` (training, recovery, mindset), `personal` (writing, learning, relationships). Everything flows into one vault. Monday morning: quick brain dump in each area. Thursdays: weekly `update` runs on whichever area needs it most.

*wiki output:* One vault, four indexed wikis. Work wiki stays sharp on client context. Business wiki distills product decisions and pricing rationale. Health wiki maps the patterns in your energy and training. Personal wiki tracks book notes and ideas. They cross-reference (e.g., *"how did the health changes affect work performance?"*) because they live in one place.

*output value:* Weekly planning that references all four wikis. Strategy docs for the business grounded in insights from work wins. Health coaching materials that incorporate lessons from your personal writing. Holistic outputs that account for whole-life context.

*Time saved / value created:* Decision-making becomes 5× faster because every life area is indexed, current, and accessible in one place — no context-switching, no re-uploading.

*Sources: SOP-C04-00 (maximum four life areas, work/business/health/personal example), SOP-C04-01 (vault root as unified location), SOP-C04-03 (weekly rhythm for all areas)*

## Why this compounds

The second brain doesn't give you a productivity boost next week. It gives you a **thinking apparatus that compounds for years**.

`raw/` fills with everything you think and observe. `wiki/` is Claude's distillation of `raw/` — the patterns, the structure, the cross-links. `output/` is where you and Claude collaborate on real work, and the best outputs fold back into the wiki. Next month, your wiki is smarter. Next quarter, it's indispensable. Next year, you've traded a chatbot that forgets last Tuesday for a knowledge base that remembers everything you've learned and surfaces it on demand.

Three canonical prompts (`build` / `update` / `health-check`), a weekly rhythm, and a system that scales from 10 notes to 10,000 without degrading. That's the compounding. Not magic — just consistency, structure, and letting Claude do the librarian work so you can focus on thinking.

## How the `/second-brain` skill uses this

After install, run `/second-brain` with no arguments. The skill will ask which persona above fits you best, load the matching case study, and tailor its first-run suggestions:

> *"Based on your **coaching** role, your `raw/` should hold post-call brain dumps, recurring objections, client examples. Your first wiki page should be a Frameworks index. Your first `output/` deliverable should be a between-session client guide. Want me to seed the first three `raw/` notes by interviewing you for one client lesson, one recurring objection, and one evergreen framework you teach?"*

You can pick a persona from the menu, paste a free-text role description if none fit, or skip the picker and just run `/second-brain build [life-area]` directly.
